**Rappel**  

*   **Definition**: To descend a steep incline, such as a cliff or building, by using a rope coiled around the body and secured to a higher point, allowing for controlled descent. It's a method of climbing down.  
*   **Common Usage**: Moderately common, primarily in contexts related to outdoor adventure sports, military operations, or emergency rescue. Less common in everyday conversation unless discussing these specific activities.  
*   **Examples**:  
    *   The firefighters were trained to rappel down the side of the burning building to rescue trapped residents.  
    *   During their adventure trip, they learned how to rappel down a waterfall.  
*   **Alternatives**: Descend, abseil (more common in British English).  

---  

**Squalor**  

*   **Definition**: A state of extreme dirtiness and unpleasantness, typically relating to living conditions. It implies a lack of hygiene, and often poverty or neglect.  
*   **Common Usage**: Not extremely common in everyday casual conversation, but frequently used in discussions about social issues, poverty, crime, or descriptive writing to convey a vivid image of very poor living conditions.  
*   **Examples**:  
    *   The news report highlighted the squalor of the slums, showing families living without basic sanitation.  
    *   His apartment had fallen into a state of squalor after he lost his job and stopped cleaning.  
*   **Alternatives**: Filth, misery, wretchedness, dilapidation.  

---  

**Deride**  

*   **Definition**: To express contempt for; to ridicule or mock. It suggests showing that you think something or someone is stupid or worthless.  
*   **Common Usage**: Fairly common in everyday language, especially when discussing criticism, arguments, or social interactions. It's a more formal word than "make fun of."  
*   **Examples**:  
    *   The politician was derided by the opposition for his controversial policy ideas.  
    *   It's unkind to deride someone's efforts, even if they aren't perfect.  
*   **Alternatives**: Mock, ridicule, scoff at, belittle, make fun of.  

---  

**Imbroglio**  

*   **Definition**: An extremely confused, complicated, or embarrassing situation. It often refers to a public entanglement or a messy dispute.  
*   **Common Usage**: Less common in daily conversation. You're more likely to encounter it in news headlines, political commentary, literary works, or when describing complex and messy public affairs or personal conflicts.  
*   **Examples**:  
    *   The company found itself in an imbroglio after several top executives were implicated in a financial scandal.  
    *   Their holiday started off fine, but quickly devolved into an imbroglio involving lost luggage, a hotel booking mix-up, and a language barrier.  
*   **Alternatives**: Mess, predicament, entanglement, quagmire, kerfuffle (more informal).  

---   

**Anodyne**  

*   **Definition**:  
    *   (Adjective) Not likely to offend or upset anyone; bland.  
    *   (Noun) A pain-killing drug or medicine.  
*   **Common Usage**: The adjective form is moderately common, especially in describing art, writing, or political statements that are deliberately mild or inoffensive. The noun form is less common in general conversation, appearing more in medical or pharmaceutical contexts.  
*   **Examples**:  
    *   The documentary was rather anodyne, lacking any strong opinions or controversial points.  
    *   He took an anodyne for his headache.  
*   **Alternatives**: (Adjective) Mild, inoffensive, bland, soothing, harmless. (Noun) Painkiller, analgesic.  

---  

**Genial**  

*   **Definition**: Friendly and cheerful.  
*   **Common Usage**: Fairly common in everyday language. It's a positive descriptor for a person's personality or demeanor.  
*   **Examples**:  
    *   The host greeted everyone with a genial smile and offered them drinks.  
    *   She had a genial nature that made it easy for her to make new friends.  
*   **Alternatives**: Friendly, cheerful, affable, cordial, amiable, warm.  

---  

**Comport**  

*   **Definition**: To behave oneself in a particular way. Often used with "oneself" or in a way that suggests maintaining decorum or dignity.  
*   **Common Usage**: Moderately common, particularly in more formal settings or when discussing behavior expected in specific social situations (e.g., at a formal event, in a professional environment). It's less likely to be used in very casual chat.  
*   **Examples**:  
    *   During the royal ceremony, the delegates were expected to comport themselves with the utmost dignity.  
    *   He did not comport himself well when he lost his temper.  
*   **Alternatives**: Behave, conduct oneself, act.  

---  

**Aegis**  

*   **Definition**: Protection, backing, or support of a particular person or organization. It's often used in the phrase "under the aegis of."  
*   **Common Usage**: Less common in everyday conversation, but frequently seen in news, professional contexts, and academic discussions to indicate sponsorship, patronage, or protection by a powerful entity.  
*   **Examples**:  
    *   The research project was conducted under the aegis of the university's science department.  
    *   The charity operates under the aegis of several influential philanthropists who provide financial backing.  
*   **Alternatives**: Protection, sponsorship, patronage, auspices, support, backing.
